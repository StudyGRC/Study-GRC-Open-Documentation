### 1. Purpose
The purpose of these guidelines is to provide Study GRC Inc. personnel with actionable best practices for setting up a safe, comfortable, and productive home work environment. As a remote-first organization, Study GRC Inc. is committed to preventing Musculoskeletal Disorders (MSDs), Repetitive Strain Injuries (RSIs), and visual fatigue. These guidelines support the Organization's commitment to safety under the General Duty Clause of the OSH Act of 1970 by addressing recognized hazards in the home office.

### 2. Scope
These guidelines apply to all **Employees**, **Contractors**, and **Volunteers** (collectively "Personnel") who perform work for Study GRC Inc. from a remote location, primarily a home office.

**Exclusions:** These guidelines are not mandatory for short-term, transient work locations (e.g., coffee shops, airports), although the principles remain beneficial.

### 3. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Ergonomics** | The applied science of equipment design intended to maximize productivity by reducing operator fatigue and discomfort. |
| **Neutral Posture** | A comfortable body position where joints are naturally aligned, reducing stress and strain on the muscles, tendons, and skeletal system. |
| **MSD** | Musculoskeletal Disorder. Injuries or disorders of the muscles, nerves, tendons, joints, cartilage, and spinal discs (e.g., Carpal Tunnel Syndrome). |
| **20-20-20 Rule** | A technique to reduce eye strain: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds. |
| **Lumbar Support** | Support for the lower back designed to maintain the natural inward curve of the spine. |

### 4. Guidelines

Personnel should utilize the following ergonomic principles to configure their home workspace. While Study GRC Inc. acknowledges that home environments vary, adhering to these guidelines significantly reduces the risk of long-term injury.

#### 4.1 Chair and Seating Position
The chair is the foundation of the workstation. Personnel should aim for the following:
*   **Height:** Adjust the seat height so feet rest flat on the floor or on a footrest. Thighs should be parallel to the floor.
*   **Lumbar Support:** The backrest should support the natural curve of the lower back. If the chair lacks built-in support, a lumbar cushion or rolled-up towel should be used.
*   **Seat Pan:** There should be a 2-3 finger gap between the front edge of the seat and the back of the knees to ensure circulation is not cut off.
*   **Armrests:** If used, armrests should allow shoulders to relax and elbows to stay close to the body. They should not force shoulders up toward the ears.

#### 4.2 Keyboard and Mouse Configuration
Input devices are the primary source of repetitive strain.
*   **Position:** Place the keyboard and mouse at elbow height. Elbows should be bent at approximately 90 degrees.
*   **Wrist Neutrality:** Wrists should remain straight, not bent up (extension) or down (flexion). Avoid planting wrists on the sharp edge of a desk.
*   **Proximity:** Keep the mouse close to the keyboard to avoid overreaching.
*   **Technique:** Type with a light touch. Avoid pounding keys.

#### 4.3 Monitor and Visual Display
Proper monitor placement prevents neck strain and headaches.
*   **Height:** The top third of the monitor screen should be at or slightly below eye level. The user should not have to tilt their head back to read the top line of text.
*   **Distance:** The monitor should be approximately an arm's length (20–28 inches) away from the face.
*   **Alignment:** The monitor should be centered directly in front of the user to prevent neck twisting.
*   **Dual Monitors:** If using two monitors, place them side-by-side in an arc. If one is primary, center the primary monitor and place the secondary one off-center.

#### 4.4 Laptop Use
Laptops are designed for portability, not ergonomic sustainability.
*   **Prolonged Use:** For work sessions exceeding one hour, a laptop **should not** be used directly on a lap or flat on a desk without modification.
*   **Risers/Stands:** Use a laptop riser or stack of books to elevate the screen to eye level.
*   **Peripherals:** When the screen is elevated, an external keyboard and mouse must be used to maintain neutral wrist posture.

#### 4.5 Lighting and Environment
*   **Glare:** Position the monitor perpendicular to windows to reduce glare. Avoid facing a window directly (contrast strain) or having a window directly behind (screen glare).
*   **Ambient Light:** Ensure the room is well-lit but not harsh. Task lighting should be used for reading paper documents.
*   **Temperature:** Maintain a comfortable temperature to prevent muscle tightening caused by cold.

#### 4.6 Movement and Micro-Breaks
The "next posture is the best posture." Static loading (sitting still) is a hazard.
*   **Frequency:** Take a micro-break (standing, stretching, or walking) every 30–60 minutes.
*   **Stretching:** Perform gentle stretches for the neck, shoulders, and wrists throughout the day.
*   **Sit-Stand Desks:** If utilizing a standing desk, alternate between sitting and standing. Standing all day can cause fatigue; a ratio of 45 minutes sitting to 15 minutes standing is often recommended.

### 5. Roles and Responsibilities

| Role | Responsibility |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Personnel (Employees/Volunteers)** | Self-assess home workspace using these guidelines. Report any persistent pain or discomfort to HR immediately. Request necessary accommodations via the *Reasonable Accommodation Request Form*. |
| **Managers** | Encourage staff to take breaks. Monitor team for signs of fatigue. Support requests for ergonomic equipment within budget constraints. |
| **Human Resources** | Provide ergonomic training materials. Review and process requests for equipment reimbursement per the *Remote Work Expense Reimbursement Policy*. |
| **IT / InfoSec** | Ensure recommended peripherals (keyboards/mice) meet security standards (e.g., encrypted wireless protocols). |

### 6. Compliance and Enforcement
While these are guidelines, failure to maintain a safe work environment may result in:
1.  Increased risk of personal injury.
2.  Denial of Workers' Compensation claims if the injury is determined to be caused by negligence or non-work-related activities.
3.  Denial of reimbursement requests for equipment that does not meet ergonomic standards.

Persistent refusal to address identified safety hazards in a remote workspace may result in a review of the individual's eligibility for remote work privileges.
